Thursday, 13 October 2016

  • Water on Europa. Elusive water plumes shooting from Jupiter’s moon Europa have revealed themselves again. Astronomers using the Hubble Space Telescope have spotted the water jets erupting three more times since they were first reported in 2013. The scientists speculate that the plumes could be tapping into an ocean buried beneath kilometres of ice. (Nature news story)

  • Pavlovian problem. In Pavlov's classic experiment, the psychologist conditioned dogs to associate food with the sound of a buzzer, leaving them salivating at the sound. Decades later, researchers discovered such training blocks animals from linking other stimuli to the same reward. But new research now suggests that this 'blocking effect' might not be so simple, after psychologists in Belgium failed to replicate the effect in 15 independent experiments. The research is part of a broader push in the social sciences to test the reliability of published results. (Nature news story; Journal of Experimental Psychology paper)

  • Farewell, Rosetta. The Rosetta comet orbiter will meet a sticky end on Friday as it crashes down to the surface of comet 67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko, bringing its 10-year mission to an end. Its grand finale should see it gather the most detailed images yet of 67P — or indeed of any comet (Nature news story). Follow the craft’s final hours live, with Rosetta mission scientists and Nature reporter Elizabeth Gibney this Friday.

  • Academy crumbling. The Zimbabwe Academy of Science is on its knees following years of chronic funding shortages and political neglect. It now has fewer than 100 fellows, and the country's economic distress means that fewer than 15 members even pay their fees. The organization has made a plea for support from the large Zimbabwean diaspora. (Nature news story)

  • Carbon quandary. A preliminary study of ten Arctic rivers suggests that they cycle roughly three to seven times more carbon through their flood plains than eventually exits the river into the ocean. The fate of that carbon isn’t known, but it represents an important chunk of the Arctic carbon budget that researchers don't yet understand. (Nature news story)

  • Poisoned air. 92% of the world’s population lives in places where air quality levels exceed limits set by the World Health Organization, with 3 million deaths a year linked to air pollution. For the first time, the WHO has broken down that figure to a country-by-country level, revealing that of the worst three nations, more than 1 million people died from dirty air in China in 2012, at least 600,000 in India and more than 140,000 in Russia. (The Guardian; WHO report)

  • Moving to Mars. Later today, Elon Musk is set to reveal his master plan for how humans can colonise Mars (Wired). The SpaceX boss is speaking at the International Astronautical Congress in Mexico, where he'll discuss the technical challenges to solve to support the creation of a "permanent, self-sustaining human presence on Mars." Watch the livestream from 14:30pm EDT / 19:30pm BST.
